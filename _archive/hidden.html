2026.03.02

Mencius wrote: 'When Heaven is about to confer a great office on any man, it first exercises his mind with suffering, and his sinews and bones with toil.' I used to think this was some kind of cosmic consolation prize—if you suffer enough, you get a medal. But that's not how it works at all.

Suffering doesn't ennoble anyone. What ennobles is what you see when suffering cracks open the world's shell. Inside, there are gears turning—laws, patterns, the very logic of how things move. And once you've seen the gears, you can't unsee them. You begin to understand what must be done.

It's like falling into a well. The fall doesn't make you wise. But at the bottom, in the dark, you finally see the water you'd been walking over all along. The path was always there—you just needed to fall to notice it.

Suffering is not the cause; it is merely the crack through which light enters.
2026.03.01

People love to say: Germany's work-life balance is why their productivity lags. Work less, produce less—simple math, right? But this gets causality exactly backwards.

The real story: Germany was an industrial pioneer. They built the machines, wrote the protocols, set the standards. But pioneers don't stay pioneers forever. Time passes, advantages erode. Factories built in the 1980s don't run like ones built in the 2020s. The machinery gets old. The margins get thin.

When productivity growth slows, there's less pie to fight over. Unions don't demand shorter hours because they're lazy—they demand them because the surplus isn't there to squeeze. You can't extract more juice from a dry lemon. So workers negotiate for time instead of money. The four-day week isn't a luxury; it's a consolation prize for an economy that's lost its edge.

Correlation is not causation. People see 'shorter hours + lower productivity' and assume A causes B. But C—structural decline—explains both. A is just the shadow.

Work-life balance isn't killing productivity. It's what productivity leaves behind when it moves somewhere else.
2026.02.28

Someone on Zhihu explained why gap years are so rare in China. The answer: gap years awaken subjectivity, and Chinese culture is fundamentally anti-subjectivity.

The logic goes like this: society has a script—you go to school, get a degree, find a job, climb the ladder, retire. Deviation is punished. Those who step off the path get labeled: selfish, aimless, unreliable. But I think there's something deeper here.

My first years of university, I was a ghost. Classes, sleep, repeat. I didn't know what I wanted. I didn't even know I was supposed to want something. The script was running me, not the other way around. I thought this was normal. I thought this was what growing up looked like.

Then 2025 happened—pain, the kind that strips you down to bolts. And in the wreckage, I finally saw myself. Not the self others wanted me to be. The actual one.

Here's what I figured out: life only has one real race—who figures out what they want first. Once you know, you can endure anything. The humiliation, the poverty, the long hours in a small room—all of it becomes bearable, even beautiful, because it's yours.

The script never cared about you. The question is: when will you stop caring about the script?

In Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, Kant declares the 'Good Will' as the only unconditional good. He distinguishes between acting 'in accordance with duty' and acting 'from duty'—true moral value lies solely in motives pure of self-interest or emotion.

The Categorical Imperative's two formulas: (1) Universal Law: 'Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.' (2) Humanity as End: 'So act that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means.'

The Trolley Problem reveals the chasm between Utilitarianism and Kantian ethics. Utilitarians sacrifice one to save five; Kant forbids treating anyone as a mere means. In the vision of AI-governed technocracy, stripped of 'absolute dignity,' ordinary people become system redundancies.

Hegel accused Kant of 'empty formalism'; Nietzsche attacked with 'will to power.' Yet Rawls' 'Veil of Ignorance' proves the practical relevance of Kant's universal law—based on rational self-interest, humanity will ultimately choose institutions that protect the weak.

Amidst the tearing between nihilism and utilitarianism, Kant's Categorical Imperative is the last line of defense code we write above the abyss: The human being must always be treated as an end, never merely as a means.

2025.12.20

Why is the unity of knowledge and action so difficult? It's mainly because we lack deep understanding of the consequences that action brings. Everyone knows that drugs and smoking are harmful to health, yet few quit because of this knowledge—they haven't felt the benefits of quitting, or in other words, they haven't deeply experienced the consequences of not quitting.

All life has the ability to adapt to its environment; living in the moment is an instinct. Most people don't consider long-term consequences. Only when the bitter fruits draw near do they sense crisis, but by then it's too late—many things in life offer no chance for trial and error. Some things do allow many trials; when pain is experienced repeatedly, one gradually achieves unity of knowledge and action in that matter. But when the pain is forgotten, one may follow impulse again...
